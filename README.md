Moiré superlattices emerge when two atomically thin layered materials, such as graphene or transition metal dichalcogenides (TMDCs), are stacked with slight twist angles or lattice mismatches, resulting in a long-wavelength moiré pattern. This pattern significantly modifies the electronic and optical properties of the original layers. Notable applications include twisted bilayer graphene, exhibiting correlated insulating states and superconductivity, and TMDCs like MoS$_2$ and WSe$_2$, where moiré patterns enhance excitonic properties, leading to localized moiré excitons with distinct optical characteristics.

Research on moiré superlattices has predominantly focused on bilayers, but recent studies are beginning to explore more complex trilayer systems with varying twist angles between layers. These configurations can produce hierarchical moiré patterns and facilitate novel electronic behaviors. Twisted trilayer graphene (TTG) can host robust flat bands pivotal for strongly correlated quantum systems. 

While experimental investigations of multilayered moiré systems are feasible, computational studies face significant challenges, particularly concerning the construction of periodic boundary condition (PBC) unit cells in density functional theory (DFT) simulations as the complexity increases. Traditional methods like TWISTER efficiently handle bilayer scenarios but are limited in applicability to more complex multilayers. 

This project presents a novel computational tool capable of constructing moiré unit cells for three or more layers with arbitrary lattice types and twist angles. By employing advanced algebraic and numerical algorithms to generate robust PBC unit cells, this code enhances the computational framework for simulating moiré phenomena, thereby facilitating the exploration of new design principles in quantum materials and devices.
